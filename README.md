<p><strong>Clay</strong>&nbsp;is a finely-grained natural&nbsp;<a title="Rock (geology)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_(geology)">rock</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a title="Soil" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil">soil</a>&nbsp;material that combines one or more&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Clay minerals" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clay_minerals">clay minerals</a>&nbsp;with possible traces of&nbsp;<a title="Quartz" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quartz">quartz</a>&nbsp;(SiO<sub>2</sub>),&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Metal oxide" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_oxide">metal oxides</a>&nbsp;(<a title="Aluminium oxide" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_oxide">Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>&nbsp;,&nbsp;<a title="Magnesium oxide" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium_oxide">MgO</a>&nbsp;etc.) and&nbsp;<a title="Organic matter" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter">organic matter</a>. Geologic clay&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Deposit (geology)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deposit_(geology)">deposits</a>&nbsp;are mostly composed of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Silicate minerals" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicate_minerals#Phyllosilicates">phyllosilicate minerals</a>&nbsp;containing variable amounts of&nbsp;<a title="Water" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water">water</a>&nbsp;trapped in the mineral structure. Clays are&nbsp;<a title="Plasticity (physics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)">plastic</a>&nbsp;due to particle size and geometry as well as water content, and become hard, brittle and non&ndash;plastic upon drying or&nbsp;<a title="Pottery" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pottery#Firing">firing</a>.&nbsp;<a title="Soil classification" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_classification">Depending on the soil's content</a>&nbsp;in which it is found, clay can appear in various colours from white to dull grey or brown to deep orange-red.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">&nbsp;</div>
<p>Although many naturally occurring deposits include both silts and clay, clays are distinguished from other fine-grained soils by differences in size and mineralogy.&nbsp;<a title="Silt" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silt">Silts</a>, which are fine-grained soils that do not include clay minerals, tend to have larger particle sizes than clays. There is, however, some overlap in particle size and other physical properties. The distinction between silt and clay varies by discipline.&nbsp;<a title="Geologist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologist">Geologists</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Soil scientist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_scientist">soil scientists</a>&nbsp;usually consider the separation to occur at a particle size of 2&nbsp;<a title="Micrometre" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre">&mu;m</a>&nbsp;(clays being finer than silts),&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentologist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedimentologist">sedimentologists</a>&nbsp;often use 4&ndash;5 &mu;m, and&nbsp;<a title="Colloid" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colloid">colloid</a>&nbsp;<a title="Chemist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemist">chemists</a>&nbsp;use 1 &mu;m.&nbsp;<a title="Geotechnical engineering" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geotechnical_engineering">Geotechnical engineers</a>&nbsp;distinguish between silts and clays based on the plasticity properties of the soil, as measured by the soils'&nbsp;<a title="Atterberg limits" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atterberg_limits">Atterberg limits</a>.&nbsp;<a title="International Organization for Standardization" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization">ISO</a>&nbsp;14688 grades clay particles as being smaller than 2 &mu;m and silt particles as being larger. Mixtures of&nbsp;<a title="Sand" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand">sand</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Silt" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silt">silt</a>&nbsp;and less than 40% clay are called&nbsp;<a title="Loam" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loam">loam</a>.</p>

<h2><span id="Formation" class="mw-headline">Formation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">&nbsp;</div>
<p>Clay minerals typically form over long periods of time as a result of the gradual chemical&nbsp;<a title="Weathering" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weathering">weathering</a>&nbsp;of rocks, usually silicate-bearing, by low concentrations of&nbsp;<a title="Carbonic acid" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonic_acid">carbonic acid</a>&nbsp;and other diluted&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Solvents" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvents">solvents</a>. These solvents, usually acidic, migrate through the weathering rock after&nbsp;<a title="Leaching (pedology)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaching_(pedology)">leaching</a>&nbsp;through upper weathered layers. In addition to the weathering process, some clay minerals are formed through&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrothermal" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal">hydrothermal</a>&nbsp;activity. There are two types of clay deposits: primary and secondary. Primary clays form as residual deposits in soil and remain at the site of formation. Secondary clays are clays that have been transported from their original location by water erosion and&nbsp;<a title="Deposition (geology)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deposition_(geology)">deposited</a>&nbsp;in a new&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedimentary">sedimentary</a>&nbsp;deposit.&nbsp;Clay deposits are typically associated with very low energy&nbsp;<a title="Depositional environment" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depositional_environment">depositional environments</a>&nbsp;such as large lakes and marine basins.</p>
<h2><span id="Grouping" class="mw-headline">Grouping</span></h2>
<p>Depending on the academic source, there are three or four main groups of clays:&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Kaolin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaolin">kaolinite</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Montmorillonite" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montmorillonite">montmorillonite</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" title="Smectite" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smectite">smectite</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Illite" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illite">illite</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Chlorite group" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorite_group">chlorite</a>. Chlorites are not always considered to be a clay, sometimes being classified as a separate group within the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Phyllosilicate" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phyllosilicate">phyllosilicates</a>. There are approximately 30 different types of "pure" clays in these categories, but most "natural" clay deposits are mixtures of these different types, along with other weathered minerals.</p>
<p><a title="Varve" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varve">Varve</a>&nbsp;(or&nbsp;<em>varved clay</em>) is clay with visible annual layers, which are formed by seasonal deposition of those layers and are marked by differences in&nbsp;<a title="Erosion" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erosion">erosion</a>&nbsp;and organic content. This type of deposit is common in former&nbsp;<a title="Glacial lake" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glacial_lake">glacial lakes</a>. When fine sediments are delivered into the calm waters of these glacial lake basins away from the shoreline, they settle to the lake bed. The resulting seasonal layering is preserved in an even distribution of clay sediment banding.</p>
<p><a title="Quick clay" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quick_clay">Quick clay</a>&nbsp;is a unique type of&nbsp;<a title="Marine clay" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_clay">marine clay</a>&nbsp;indigenous to the glaciated terrains of&nbsp;<a title="Norway" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway">Norway</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Canada" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada">Canada</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Northern Ireland" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Sweden" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden">Sweden</a>. It is a highly sensitive clay, prone to&nbsp;<a title="Soil liquefaction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_liquefaction">liquefaction</a>, which has been involved in several deadly&nbsp;<a title="Landslide" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landslide">landslides</a>.</p>

<h2><span id="Historical_and_modern_uses" class="mw-headline">Historical and modern uses</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">&nbsp;</div>
<div class="thumb tright">&nbsp;</div>
<p>Clays exhibit plasticity when mixed with water in certain proportions. However, when dry, clay becomes firm and when fired in a&nbsp;<a title="Kiln" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiln">kiln</a>, permanent physical and chemical changes occur. These changes convert the clay into a&nbsp;<a title="Ceramic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceramic">ceramic</a>&nbsp;material. Because of these properties, clay is used for making&nbsp;<a title="Pottery" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pottery">pottery</a>, both utilitarian and decorative, and construction products, such as bricks, wall and floor tiles. Different types of clay, when used with different minerals and firing conditions, are used to produce&nbsp;<a title="Earthenware" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthenware">earthenware</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Stoneware" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoneware">stoneware</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Porcelain" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porcelain">porcelain</a>. Prehistoric humans discovered the useful properties of clay. Some of the earliest pottery shards recovered are from central&nbsp;<a title="Honshu" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honshu">Honshu</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Japan" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan">Japan</a>. They are associated with the&nbsp;<a title="Jōmon period" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C5%8Dmon_period">Jōmon</a>&nbsp;culture, and recovered deposits have been&nbsp;<a title="Radiocarbon dating" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating">dated</a>&nbsp;to around 14,000&nbsp;BC.&nbsp;Cooking pots, art objects, dishware,&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Smoking pipe (tobacco)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smoking_pipe_(tobacco)">smoking pipes</a>, and even&nbsp;<a title="Musical instrument" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_instrument">musical instruments</a>&nbsp;such as the&nbsp;<a title="Ocarina" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocarina">ocarina</a>&nbsp;can all be shaped from clay before being fired.</p>
<p>Clay tablets were the first known writing medium.&nbsp;Scribes wrote by inscribing them with&nbsp;<a title="Cuneiform" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuneiform">cuneiform</a>&nbsp;script using a blunt&nbsp;<a title="Reed (plant)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reed_(plant)">reed</a>&nbsp;called a&nbsp;<a title="Stylus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stylus">stylus</a>. Purpose-made clay balls were used as&nbsp;<a title="Sling (weapon)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sling_(weapon)#Ammunition">sling ammunition</a>. Clay is used in many industrial processes, such as&nbsp;<a title="Paper" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper">paper</a>&nbsp;making,&nbsp;<a title="Cement" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cement">cement</a>&nbsp;production, and chemical&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Filter (chemistry)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filter_(chemistry)">filtering</a>. Until the late 20th century,&nbsp;<a title="Bentonite" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bentonite">bentonite</a>&nbsp;clay was widely used as a mold binder in the manufacture of&nbsp;<a title="Sand casting" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_casting">sand castings</a>.</p>
<p>Clay, being relatively&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Permeability (fluid)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permeability_(fluid)">impermeable</a>&nbsp;to water, is also used where&nbsp;<a title="Puddling (civil engineering)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puddling_(civil_engineering)">natural seals</a>&nbsp;are needed, such as in the cores of&nbsp;<a title="Dam" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dam">dams</a>, or as a barrier in&nbsp;<a title="Landfill" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landfill">landfills</a>&nbsp;against toxic seepage (lining the landfill, preferably in combination with&nbsp;<a title="Geotextile" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geotextile">geotextiles</a>).&nbsp;Studies in the early 21st century have investigated clay's&nbsp;<a title="Sorption" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sorption">absorption</a>&nbsp;capacities in various applications, such as the removal of&nbsp;<a title="Heavy metals" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_metals">heavy metals</a>&nbsp;from waste water and air purification.</p>
<h3><span id="Medical_use" class="mw-headline">Medical use</span></h3>
<p>Traditional uses of&nbsp;<a title="Medicinal clay" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicinal_clay">clay as medicine</a>&nbsp;goes back to prehistoric times. An example is&nbsp;<a title="Armenian bole" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_bole">Armenian bole</a>, which is used to soothe an upset stomach. Some animals such as parrots and pigs ingest clay for similar reasons.&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Kaolin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaolin">Kaolin clay</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Attapulgite" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attapulgite">attapulgite</a>&nbsp;have been used as anti-diarrheal medicines.</p>
<h3><span id="As_a_building_material" class="mw-headline">As a building material</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a title="Edit section: As a building material" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">&nbsp;</div>
<p>Clay as the defining ingredient of&nbsp;<a title="Loam" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loam">loam</a>&nbsp;is one of the oldest building materials on&nbsp;<a title="Earth" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth">Earth</a>, among other ancient, naturally-occurring geologic materials such as stone and organic materials like wood.&nbsp;Between one-half and two-thirds of the world's population, in both traditional societies as well as developed countries, still live or work in buildings made with clay, often baked into brick, as an essential part of its load-bearing structure. Also a primary ingredient in many&nbsp;<a title="Natural building" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_building">natural building</a>&nbsp;techniques, clay is used to create&nbsp;<a title="Adobe" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adobe">adobe</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Cob (material)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cob_(material)">cob</a>,&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Cordwood" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cordwood">cordwood</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Rammed earth" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rammed_earth">rammed earth</a>&nbsp;structures and building elements such as&nbsp;<a title="Wattle and daub" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wattle_and_daub">wattle and daub</a>, clay plaster, clay render case, clay floors and clay&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Paints" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paints">paints</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Ceramic building material" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceramic_building_material">ceramic building material</a>. Clay was used as a&nbsp;<a title="Mortar (masonry)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)">mortar</a>&nbsp;in brick&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Chimneys" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chimneys">chimneys</a>&nbsp;and stone walls where protected from water.</p>
